7 foreign programming blogs from student days

TechnologyIdeasRepostsDeveloper LifeIdeas
If you want to become a programmer, you need to immerse yourself in programming culture. This is even more true if you are still a student. The field of programming is so vast and absorbs so much information that if you participate from afar, you'll never be on top. Fortunately, blogging is an easily accessible medium. Just be clear, these programming blogs are not meant to replace hands-on practice or proper instruction. If you are a complete newbie, you should first choose a programming language to learn and then start by learning the basics. Once you get your feet wet, that's where these blogs will come in handy and improve your skills in a few notches. ①Joel on Software（CEO of Stack Overflow） Joel on Softwareis one of the most incredible resources for any aspiring software developer. Over the past 15 years, Joel Spolsky has written and amassed over 1,100 different articles on topics ranging from coding habits to project management, software business practices to the emergence of new technologies. For your benefit, Joel has a clear list of his "Top Ten" articles for easy browsing. He also provides some helpful articles for "new developers" that discuss the mindset of a novice programmer, how to progress when you feel like sucking, and some other must-read advice. Unfortunately, Joel retired last year and no longer writes new articles for the site. However, the summary of knowledge is so great that you have to pass it on. ②Coding Horror Coding Horroris a semi-personal blog actively maintained by experienced web and software developer Jeff Atwood. You'll find programming-related posts, your own private thoughts on a topic, summaries of his own research, and even advice for new and experienced programmers alike. Blog posts have evolved over the years and the focus of blogs has evolved with them. That being said, the posts are relatively light and they will make you think. ③Gamasutra If you are interested in game development, or want to get into the gaming industry, Gamasutra is one of the websites you must read. Profit for free? VR console? Late in development? Recommended for successful independence? Need help finding a job? You'll find all this and more here. ④List Apart List Apartis a multi-author blog focusing on web design, web development, web standards and web content. Basically, if there's anything important happening in the world of network programming, you're probably going to hear about it here. That's not to say it's a news site - because it's not - but it's an important resource. Lots of good advice here. What tools are useful for web developers? What's the best way to style elements? Are you interested in interviewing a web development expert? List Apart is updated at least once a week, so it slowly but surely fills up the web with goodwill content. If you have a writing chop, you can also write for them and get paid. ⑤David Walsh Blog The David Walsh Blog is in addition to the same list of other web development focused blogs, but its focus is slightly different. The format of these posts is more of a tutorial with specific goals and examples than a lofty theory and design guide. This is a great learning resource for those new to website development. ⑥Girl Developer It's no secret that programmers clubs are dominated by men, but it becomes less true every year. Yet the stigma behind “female coders”areWorsening, but that doesn't mean it's extinct. It's hard being a woman in a man's world, here's the thinggirl developerThe reason why blogs exist. Girl Develop It is run by Sara Chipps, a successful web coder who co-founded Girl Develop It, a nonprofit that helps women become software developers. Her blog touches on gender-neutral topics from time to time, but it's a great window into some of her challenges and struggles. ⑦Daily WTF Daily WTF is a user-submitted blog that showcases some of the worst coding practices seen in real work environments. Site owners approve and edit submissions to maintain consistency between articles, so don't let the "User Submissions" section turn you away. Fortunately, the point of the site isn't a mockery of bad code, but a warning about what to expect in the real world. There's also a common misery because we, the readers, sympathize with the committers having to deal with any erroneous code. Why should you read it? Because you will learn what not to do. Because no matter how bad you think you are as a programmer, at least you're not such a bad person. And because it's humorous!
