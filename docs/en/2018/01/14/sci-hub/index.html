Sci-Hub

IdeasDeveloper LifeIdeas
Sci-Hub is an online database that provides 62,000,000 scientific academic papers and articles. The website accesses relevant pages through the ".edu" proxy server, and new papers and articles are uploaded every day. In 2011, Kazakh graduate student Alexandra Elbakyan decided to establish Sci-Hub because the cost of research papers was too high, with each paper usually costing $30 under the paywall mechanism. In 2014, academics began to predict that the website would evolve into a Napster-like service. However, in 2015, academic publisher Elsevier filed a lawsuit with the New York District Court, accusing Sci-Hub of copyright infringement.Founder:Alexandra Elbakyan

history

Sci-Hub was founded by Alexandra Elbakyan, a Kazakh software developer and neurotechnology researcher, and was officially launched on September 5, 2011. Among them, when Elbakyan was conducting research at Harvard University, he used infringing papers shared by others because he could not afford the hundreds of papers he needed. This experience made her believe that only by allowing more people to browse the content of the paywall mechanism can the spread of knowledge be improved. Previously, requests for use in research papers had to be made by emailing the authors or other academics directly. Other ways to obtain papers are through users of online research forums, or social networking services such as Twitter (ICanHazPDF). Since 2013, Sci-Hub has cooperated with Library Genesis - LibGen, a Russian educational book and archive database. The Sci-Hub website is also becoming popular in developing countries such as India, Indonesia, Pakistan, Iran, the People's Republic of China, Russia, and Brazil. Among them, in the original goal of creating the website, Elbakyan clearly mentioned that it would provide research materials for institutions and countries with fewer resources. In 2014, academics began to predict that the website would develop into a service similar to Napster. The original domain name of the project was Sci-Hub.org, which had an average of more than 80,000 visitors per day before it was banned by a US court.

mechanism

Sci-Hub is the first known website to provide a large number of automatic and free paid academic papers. Users do not need to subscribe or pay in advance to use papers originally stored in paid databases, and provide search services for document files on the original publisher's website. These files are used through accounts provided by anonymous scholars around the world. Through these accounts, the website can obtain and assemble papers owned by JSTOR, Springer Science + Business Media, Sage Publishing, Elsevier and other publishers. When a user submits a paper request to Sci-Hub, the website will first search whether the Genesis Library has available files; if the Genesis Library has not stored it in advance, Sci-Hub will download the article under a specific database account and send it to the Genesis Library for backup for future use.

legal dispute

In 2015, Elsevier filed an infringement lawsuit against Sci-Hub in the U.S. District Court in New York. Elsevier claimed that Sci-Hub illegally obtained articles on its own platform ScienceDirect through the accounts of students and academic institutions, and provided more people with free access. Since the Sci-Hub website is actually registered in St. Petersburg, Russia, it is difficult for U.S. legal agencies to take action and the case is therefore more complicated. At the same time, some people questioned Elsevier's attempt to cooperate with Wikipedia. The motive behind it was to promote papers that adopted a paid system. In addition, the Genesis Library, registered in the Netherlands and Russia, was also accused by Elsevier. In this regard, Elbakyan cited Article 27 of the United Nations' Universal Declaration of Human Rights: "Everyone has the right to freely participate in the cultural life of society, appreciate art, and share in scientific progress and its benefits." The lawsuit led to various criticisms of Elsevier, and the Electronic Frontier Foundation also publicly supported the actions of Sci-Hub and Genesis Library.On October 28, 2015, the New York District Court ordered that the domain name "Sci-Hub.org" originally used by Sci-Hub must be terminated. After Elsevier won the lawsuit in court, a group of researchers, writers and artists signed an open letter expressing support for Sci-Hub and Genesis Library, claiming that the lawsuit was a "major blow" to researchers around the world, and stated: "It also demeans us, authors, editors and readers. It is parasitic on our labor, it prevents us from serving the public, and it blocks our access." After Sci-Hub.org was suspended due to a court order in November, it was re-launched within the same month using the domain name sci-hub.io and enabling the Tor hidden service scihub22266oqcxt.onion. In January 2016, Sci-Hub had an average of about 200,000 visits per day [11], and Sci-Hub claimed that the website served an average of hundreds of thousands of file requests every day.

Comment:

Accessing papers is an urgent need for every scientific researcher, but if you want to be a scholar, you must first "pay tolls" to several publishing groups. JSTOR, Springer, Sage, and Elsevier, these publishers almost monopolize 99% of the world’s academic paper resources. Among them, Elsevier (also the protagonist of this case) owns more than 2,000 academic journals including The Lancet. It can be said that if anyone wants to stay in the scientific community, it is impossible not to deal with them. Although publishers operate journals, it is natural for them to charge for reading. But as the saying goes, "Under a monopoly, there must be huge profits." When the capitalists discovered that there was no competition in the paper market, high prices became almost an inevitable result. Academic papers have become a profit-making tool for a few publishing giants. Taking Elsevier as an example, the price of an ordinary magazine is US$13.74, and it often only supports one computer for viewing. A one-year subscription to electronic journals can cost hundreds or thousands of dollars, and the most expensive journal can even cost as much as $40,000! Harvard University alone spends $3.75 million a year on its literature database. Zhejiang University spends 20 million yuan of its annual library construction fee of 30 million yuan on purchasing foreign language databases. It’s outrageous that even so, publishers are still not satisfied and are still raising prices every year! From 2008 to now, Elsevier has raised prices in China by an average of 16.7% every year, which has made Chinese universities including Tsinghua and Peking University miserable. Someone has calculated that Apple's profit margin was about 23.4% in 2011. In the past 10 years, Elsevier's profit margin has never been less than 30%, and it was as high as 37.3% in 2011.
