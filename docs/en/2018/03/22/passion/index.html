What to do if you lose your passion for life?

IdeasReposts随笔Ideas
This article originated from Emberton, the answerer on Quora.15K+ likes) Many people believe in a miracle called "enthusiasm." “If only I could find my passion,” we cry, “I will only be happy if I find my passion.” Indeed, passion is real and powerful. But most people have the wrong idea about "finding."

Rule 1: Passion comes from success

All the emotions we have exist for a valid reason. We feel hungry in order not to starve to death. We feel full so we don’t die suddenly. We feel passionate so that we can focus on the things that will bring us the greatest reward. Let's say you join a dance class. You think dancing is easy. You find that you dance better and learn faster than others. That spontaneous excitement is your passion, and that passion makes you pay more to improve your skills and strengthen your strength.
The enemy of enthusiasm is depression. If you struggle with something for a long time, you will never develop a passion for it. You will learn to avoid it completely, and of course you will never improve.Most people think the other way around. They believe that we must first find our passion, which makes us good at something. In fact, you should first look for something you are good at. Passion comes from success.

Rule 2: Childhood is where passion is buried

In theory, childhood provides a great opportunity to try a variety of things, find your talents, and then discover your passion. But think about it and you’ll see how harmful this theory can be to you. The school lets you try out 20 subjects and then ranks your results among thousands of kids. The odds of winning are not high. Most children are, by definition, around average. This does not mean that our education level has improved much, because people need to feel that they are extraordinary before they can feel enthusiastic, and improving education level only improves the average level.Maybe you are one of those lucky guys who was the best in math in your middle school. Yet the education system keeps making things harder for you until you find a level, like college, where you are no longer extraordinary. Even if you're objectively really good, once you feel like you're just average, you'll find that your enthusiasm wanes. If you are lucky. What if you have a passion for art? Then from a very young age, your enthusiasm will have to compromise with social rules. "It's hard to support yourself by painting," your parents say. "Your cousin is so good at engineering. Can't you just learn from him?" Then you put your passion aside and let them dry up. Among billions of people, it's clear that not everyone can do unusually well in a bunch of academic subjects. Suppose you are really good at speaking, writing or creative dance, or making game explanation videos on YouTube. None of these things are in the syllabus. So most people don't find that they have much passion for anything when they grow up.

Rule three: Passion can be created

The passions of most successful people in life were not found on a bookshelf.
In fact, most of the world's successful people have dropped out of education entirely. Not because they're stupid - it's because they find education doesn't recognize the areas in which they excel. They create their own passion. Only a very small percentage of the population can expect to excel in the few subjects that are taught to us in childhood. The competition in that space was, so to speak, “everyone in the world who goes to school,” which didn’t really help our odds. But if you step outside this space, you will find less competition and more choices. This will increase your chances of finding your passion.

Choose one:create

When you create something new, you are inventing your passion. You could design a novelty cushion, or write a Batman story, or run a Twitter account focused on flawed politicians.
There is relatively no competition for new things. By creating something new, you make your odds much, much better. Please note that this does not violate Rule 1: Passion comes from success. So if your new Twitter account only has 5 followers a year later, you probably won’t be too enthusiastic about it. But if you had 5 million followers, you would quit your job. You have to find success to sustain your passion.
But at least you greatly improve your odds because the competition you face is so limited. But only a few people dare to try something new. But you can become one of them, starting now. You can verify this pattern by looking back at history. A student named Mark will never become the best programmer in the world. But he set out to build a cool website, and he discovered that he was really good at it because even programmers better than him rarely dared to try it. And so his little experiment became Facebook.

Option 2: Lead the new trend

The longer and more established a field is, the harder it is to win because tens of millions of people have been there before you. The lower your odds of being great, the lower your odds of becoming more passionate. But there will always be a frontier, a field where no one can hope to be competent, where even modest talent can be amazing.
Let’s go back to 2005 and imagine you’re a teenager just starting out making YouTube videos. The amount of attention you get is okay, but continued success spurs you on. As the "growing" world realizes that YouTube is an amazing thing with 4 billion views a day, you have become a passionate powerhouse with a new skill that is infinitely valuable. This is not a pipe dream. There are tons of people who have become hugely successful on YouTube, and most of them did it the same way: by getting ahead of everyone else. This is the same for the first person to write a blog, play rap, and design games. If you can identify new things that are developing rapidly and develop skills in this area before you can, you will find that it is not difficult to win in this area due to the lack of competition. That will be your new passion.

Option 3: Integrate mediocre talents

One of the barriers to education is that it is designed to limit your skills. Education usually finds the one thing you are best at and then pushes it as far as you can bear.
The problem is that most of us, by definition, can’t be the best at one area. But when we combine our strengths, we can become very good. Suppose you are a mediocre artist with a great sense of humor. You won't have much attainment at the artistic level, and you can't take the study of "humor" as a theme. But you can become a great cartoonist. Or you're a mediocre business student with programming skills and great sales skills. Then you're ideally suited to be the boss of guys who are better than you in one of those areas. The most successful people are never limited to one skill. They combine a variety of skills, often not excelling in any one, but he blends these skills into excellence. Jobs was not the world's greatest engineer, salesperson, designer or businessman. But what's unique is that he was good enough at each of those things and then weaved those skills together into something much greater. This is your final path to finding your passion: combine your skills into something more valuable. Remember, passion breeds success. If the new blend gives you good results, then it's your passion. Why Passion Matters Passion is a fascinating thing. When passion is born from something you believe you are particularly good at, you become passionate and you say, "By the way, I am awesome" in a very sincere way.
Enthusiasm will convince people to follow you and convince people to believe in you. But most importantly, passion can convince you. Passion drives you crazy and drives you to work obsessively because your brain believes it can rock your world. It's like love, a feeling worth fighting for. It's like love, it's so important that it can make us ignore our fate. If you haven't found your passion yet, create something new, set a new trend, and combine what you're good at.
