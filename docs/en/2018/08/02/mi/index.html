Mutual information formula and overview

TechnologyMachine Learning信息论
In probability theory and information theory, the relationship between two random variablesMutual Information (MI)Or transfer information (transinformation) is a measure of interdependence between variables. Unlike the correlation coefficient, mutual information is not limited to real-valued random variables, it is more general and determines how similar the joint distribution p(X,Y) and the product of decomposed marginal distributions p(X)p(Y) are. Mutual information (Mutual Information) measures the correlation (mutual dependence) between two event sets. The most commonly used unit of mutual information is bit.

Intuitively, mutual information measures the information shared by X and Y: it measures how much knowing one of the two variables reduces uncertainty about the other. For example, if X and Y are independent of each other, then knowing that X provides no information about Y and vice versa, their mutual information is zero. At the other extreme, if X is a deterministic function of Y, and Y is also a deterministic function of Therefore, the mutual information in this case is the same as the uncertainty contained in Y (or X) alone, called the entropy of Y (or X). Moreover, this mutual information is the same as the entropy of X and the entropy of Y. (A very special case of this is when X and Y are the same random variables.) Mutual information is the intrinsic dependence between the joint distribution of X and Y relative to the joint distribution if X and Y are assumed to be independent. Mutual information then measures dependence in the following way: I(X; Y) = 0 if and only if X and Y are independent random variables.
