Introduction to Boolean Algebra

A mathematical model proposed by British mathematician G. Boole in 1847 and 1854 in order to study the laws of thinking (logic, mathematical logic). The so-called Boolean algebra refers to an ordered four-tuple 〈B,∨,∧,*〉, where B is a non-empty set, ∨ and ∧ are two binary operations defined on B, * is a unary operation defined on B, and they meet certain conditions. Due to the lack of physical background, Boolean algebra research was slow. It was not until the 1930s and 1940s that new progress was made. In 1935, M.H. Stone first pointed out that there is a clear connection between Boolean algebra and rings. He also obtained what is now called Stone's representation theorem: any Boolean algebra must be isomorphic to a set field on a certain set; any Boolean algebra must also be isomorphic to Closed and open algebra of a certain topological space, etc., which has led to a certain development of Boolean algebra in theory. Boolean algebra is applied in algebra (algebraic structure), logical calculus, set theory, topological space theory, measure theory, probability theory, functional analysis and other branches of mathematics. After 1967, he also played a certain role in the theoretical research of axiomatic set theory and model theory, one of the branches of mathematical logic. In recent decades, Boolean algebra hasAutomation technology, logic design of electronic computersIt has important applications in other fields of engineering technology.

George Boole and a brief history of Boolean algebra

George Boole (George Boole, 1815.11.2 ~ 1864) was born on November 2, 1815 in Lincoln, England. One of the most important mathematicians of the 19th century published Mathematical Analysis of Logic, the first of many contributions to symbolic logic. In 1854, he published A Study of the Laws of Thought, his most famous work. In this book Boole introduced the Boolean algebra that now bears his name. ——Baidu Encyclopedia In 1835, 20-year-old George Boole opened a private teaching school. In order to provide students with necessary mathematics courses, he read with great interest some textbooks that introduced mathematics knowledge at that time. Soon, he was surprised, were these things mathematics? It was unbelievable. Therefore, this young man with only preliminary mathematical training taught himself the difficult "Celestial Mechanics" and the very abstract "Analytical Mechanics". Because he had a strong feeling for the symmetry and beauty of algebraic relations, in his lonely research, he first discovered invariants and wrote this result as a paper for publication. After the publication of this high-quality paper, Boole still stayed in teaching in primary schools, but he began to interact with many first-class There were many British mathematicians who interacted or corresponded, including the mathematician and logician De Morgan. Morgan was involved in a famous controversy in the first half of the 19th century. Boole knew that Morgan was right, so he published a thin pamphlet in 1848 to defend his friend. This book was a preview of his greater things six years later. When it came out, it immediately aroused Morgan's praise and affirmed that he had opened up new and thorny research subjects. Boole was already studying logical algebra, that is, Boolean algebra. He reduced logic to an extremely easy and simple algebra. In this algebra, "reasoning" on appropriate materials became a matter of elementary operations of formulas. These formulas were much simpler than most of the formulas used in the second-year middle school algebra course. In this way, logic itself was dominated by mathematics. In order to perfect his research work, Boole made extraordinary efforts over the next six years.In 1854, he published the masterpiece "Laws of Thinking". At that time, he was 39 years old. Boolean algebra was born, setting a new milestone in the history of mathematics. Like almost all new things, Boolean algebra was not taken seriously by people after its invention.Famous mathematicians on the European continent disdainfully called it something that had no mathematical meaning and was philosophically bizarre. They doubted that mathematicians from the British Isles could make unique contributions to mathematics. Boole died shortly after the publication of his masterpiece. At the beginning of the 20th century, Russell believed in "Principles of Mathematics" that "pure mathematics was discovered by Boole in a work he called "The Laws of Thinking"." As soon as this statement was made, it immediately attracted the world's attention to Boolean algebra. Today, logical algebra invented by Boolean has developed into a major branch of pure mathematics. In discrete mathematics, Boolean algebra (sometimes called Boolean grid) is a complementary lattice (see the definition of lattice). The elements can be thought of in various ways; the most common is to think of them as a Generalized truth values. As a simple example, suppose there are three conditions that are independently true or false. The elements of Boolean algebra can then specify exactly those that are true; then Boolean algebra itself will be a collection of all eight possibilities, and with them the ways of combining them. A related topic sometimes called Boolean algebra is Boolean logic, which can be defined as something common to all Boolean algebras. It consists of relationships that always hold between the elements of a Boolean algebra, regardless of which Boolean algebra you have. Because the algebra of logic gates and some electronic circuits is also formally like this, the same is true of mathematical logic. As in this series, Boolean logic is also studied in engineering and computer science. The operations on Boolean algebra are called AND, OR, and NOT. For the algebraic structure to be Boolean, these operations must behave like a two-element Boolean algebra (the two elements are TRUE and FALSE).

Boole's inspiration for Shannon

Shannon obtained a master's degree in electrical engineering from MIT in 1938. The title of his master's thesis was "Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switch Circuits". This symbolic analysis completely used the Boolean algebra system. He noticed the similarity between telephone exchanges and Boolean algebra, and correlated the true and false of Boolean algebra with the switches in the circuit system, and represented them with 1 and 0. The telephones at that time were far from the telephones now, and they were completely different.
