Common data enhancement strategies in contrastive learning

TechnologyMachine LearningDeep LearningKnowledge GraphsNatural Language Processing计算机视觉神经网络Natural Language ProcessingKnowledge GraphsGNN计算机视觉
[latexpage]

contrastive learning

Contrastive learning is a self-supervised learning method that learns representations by comparing positive and negative examples. For self-supervised contrastive learning, the next equation is the contrastive loss: \[ \mathcal{L}_{i,j} = - \log \frac{exp(\textbf{z}_i \cdot \textbf{z}_j / \tau)}{\sum_{k=1,k\neq i}^{2N}exp(\textbf{z}_i \cdot \textbf{z}_k / \tau)} \] In many cases, contrastive learning only needs to generate a positive sample for each sample, and other samples in the same batch serve as negative samples.

image

In the field of images, the design of positive samples is relatively simple. The first picture here is the original image, and the other pictures are positive samples obtained by rotating, cropping, covering, adding noise, etc. Therefore, in the image domain, unsupervised contrastive learning is often easier to train.

NLP

In the field of natural language processing, there are also many ways to enhance positive samples, these are two of them: One is synonym substitution（Synonym substitution）, we can use bert or word2vec to find words that are similar in the semantic space, and replace the original words for data enhancement. Another technique is called post-translation（Back-translation）, for example, if I want to enhance English data, then I can use Google or Youdao to translate it into French, Chinese, etc., and then translate French and Chinese back to English. In this way I also achieved data enhancement.

Graph

In the field of graph, data enhancement methods are relatively limited. There are currently several mainstream methods, including point removal, edge perturbation and feature shuffle. For example, we can remove some less important nodes from the graph, or we can use neighborhood information to perform edge perturbation heuristically.

Knowledge Graph

In terms of knowledge graphs, there are few works based on contrastive learning. Currently, I have found one article, which uses triplet loss instead of the mainstream InfoNCE, and it constructs negative samples. For a triplet, the article uses K-order neighbors as negative samples. For example, for this red central node, its first-order neighbors, that is, the yellow nodes, are positive samples. The 2-3-4-order neighbors have no edges connected to the red intermediate node, so they can be used as negative samples.
