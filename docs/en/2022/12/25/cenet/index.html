Time series knowledge mapping reasoning

TKG has provided new perspectives and insights for many downstream applications, such as decision-making, stock forecasting and dialogue systems, thus generating strong interest in TKG reasoning. In doing so, we have focused on predicting future events on TKG. In order to model the structure and time characteristics of TKG for future event predictions, some mainstream models, such as RE-NET, can easily predict repetitive or cyclical events using a self-return approach. However, in the TKG dataset ICEWS (Integrated Crisis Early Warning System), about 40 per cent of new events occur. The prediction of these new events is very challenging because they have fewer interactive traces on the historical timeline. For example, the right part of figure 2 shows the query.（the United States, Negotiate, ?, t+1）and its corresponding new events（the United States, Negotiate, Russia, t+1）Most of the existing methods generally yield the wrong results in such inquiries because of the excessive attention paid to frequent repeat events. Problems with existing models: In the reasoning process, on the other hand, the current methodology ranked all the candidate entities in the overall chart with a probability score, without any beas. We believe that this type of bias is necessary in dealing with the missing entities of different events, as is the case in the real world, for example, in the case of repetitive or cyclical events, where priority should be given to entities that are frequently occurring, while in the case of new events, more attention needs to be paid to entities with less historical interaction.

Time series knowledge mapping reasoning based on historical comparisons

In doing so, we will go beyond the limitations of historical information to exploit potential time-series patterns from the whole body of knowledge. For the sake of a clearer description of our design, we refer to past events associated with the entities in the current query as historical events of the query and other events as non-historical events. Intuitively, we believe that the TKG events are not only related to its historical events, but are also indirectly related to non-observed potential factors. Experiment

Dataset

We chose five baseline data sets, including three incident-based TKG and two public KGs. These two types of data sets are constructed in different ways. The first three, based on international political events, comprised ICEWS18, ICEWS14 and GDELT, were separated. The events in the last two public KGs (WIKI and YAGO) may have continued for a long time.

Conclusions

In this paper, we have proposed a new time-series knowledge spectrograph model for event prediction - CENET. The key idea of CENET is to learn a convincing distribution of the whole set of entities and to identify key entities from historical and non-historical dependence in a comparative learning framework. The results of the experiment show that CENET is significantly superior to all available methods on most indicators, especially Hits@1. Future work includes exploring comparative learning skills in knowledge maps, such as finding more rational comparative learning pairs.
