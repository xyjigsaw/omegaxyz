A review of research on solving NP hard problems based on evolutionary computation

TechnologyMachine LearningEvolutionary AlgorithmsNSGA-IIPSO算法特征选择人工智能

pickwant

As a stochastic optimization algorithm, evolutionary algorithm has been widely used in the fields of complex function optimization, combinatorial optimization and path planning. This article conducts a detailed analysis and investigation from the development status, defects and improvements of evolutionary algorithms. The definition and research results of NP problems are introduced in detail, and methods and strategies for solving NP problems with constrained combinatorial optimization based on traditional classic and latest cutting-edge evolutionary algorithms are studied and discussed. Experimental results on standard data sets show that evolutionary algorithms have certain practicability and scalability in solving NP problems.Keywords:Evolutionary computation; NP-hard problem; genetic algorithm; swarm intelligence; multi-objective optimizationA Review of NP Problem Solving Based on Evolutionary Computation Abstract: As a stochastic optimization algorithm, evolutionary algorithm is widely used in the fields of complex function optimization, combinatorial optimization and path planning. This paper makes a detailed analysis and investigation on the development status, defects and improvement of evolutionary algorithms. The definition and research results of NP problem are introduced in detail while the methods and strategies of solving constrained NP problem based on classical and latest evolutionary algorithms are studied and discussed in combinatorial optimization. Experimental results on the standard data set show that the evolutionary algorithm is practical and extensible in solving NP hard problems. Key Words: evolutionary computing; NP problem; genetic algorithm; swarm intelligence; multi-objective optimization

1. Evolutionary calculation

1.1 The history and current situation of evolutionary computation

In the 1950s, when computer performance was far behind, Darwin's theory of evolution was applied to automated problem solving.[1]. In the United States, Fogel proposed the idea of ​​evolutionary programming [2], which was later called the genetic algorithm (GA). The GA algorithm is influenced by the natural selection mechanism of "survival of the fittest" and the transmission law of genetic information in the process of biological evolution. It simulates this process iteratively through the program, regards the problem to be solved as the environment, and seeks the optimal solution through natural evolution in a population composed of some possible solutions.[3], these theories developed independently for about 15 years. Before the 1980s, it did not attract much attention because it was not mature enough and was limited by the small capacity and slow computing speed of computers at that time, and it did not develop practical application results. By the early 1990s, genetic programming (GP)[4]was also proposed, so evolutionary computing, represented by genetic algorithms, genetic programming, evolutionary strategies and evolutionary planning, began to officially appear as a discipline, integrating new evolutionary algorithms and promoting great development in the field of machine learning. At the same time, based on the ideas of Darwin's theory of evolution, evolutionary algorithms are emerging one after another and are in the ascendant, such as the particle swarm algorithm (PSO).[5], Ant Colony Algorithm (ACO)[6], Artificial Bee Colony Algorithm (ABC)[7]etc. These algorithms are all stochastic optimization algorithms based on swarm intelligence and are widely used in many fields.

1.2 Traditional evolutionary computation classification

There are four categories of traditional evolutionary computation, namely genetic algorithms, genetic programming, evolutionary strategies and evolutionary planning.[1]. In this section, we will focus on genetic algorithms. The genetic algorithm starts from a population that represents a set of possible potential solutions to the problem, and a population is composed of a certain number of individuals that are genetically encoded (generally including real number encoding and binary encoding). Each individual is actually an entity with characteristic chromosomes. As the main carrier of genetic material, chromosomes are a collection of multiple genes. Their internal expression is a certain combination of genes, which determines the external expression of the individual's shape. After the first-generation population is generated, in accordance with the principles of survival of the fittest and survival of the fittest, successive generations of evolution produce better and better approximate solutions. In each generation, individuals are selected according to their fitness in the problem domain, and combined crossover and mutation are performed with the help of genetic operators of natural genetics to generate a population that represents a new solution set. This process will lead to a population that is more adaptable to the environment than the previous generation, like natural evolution. The optimal individual in the last generation population can be decoded and used as an approximate optimal solution to the problem. There is the classic GA algorithm in the single-objective field, and the NSGA2 algorithm based on fast non-dominated sorting and crowding distance selection in the multi-objective field.[8]. The essence of genetic programming is that using generalized hierarchical computer programs to describe problems is more suitable for solving a class of complex nonlinear problems caused by various uncertain factors.

1.3 Algorithms based on swarm intelligence (represented by PSO)

Because evolutionary computing has strategic flexibility and adaptability to problems, it is often used to solve optimization problems in science and engineering fields. Evolutionary computing usually has great advantages over classical optimization algorithms. Among them, the PSO algorithm based on swarm intelligence was proposed by Kennedy and Eberhart, which is a stochastic optimization algorithm.[5]. In the particle swarm algorithm, each particle has a position vector and a velocity vector. The position vector represents a candidate solution, and the velocity vector represents the speed and direction that the particle needs to move in the next generation. Each vector consists of n real values, where n is the dimension of the current problem. Each particle estimates its degree of excellence through the fitness function value. The optimal solution searched by each particle is called pbest (local optimal solution), and the optimal solution searched in all generations of the entire particle swarm is called gbest (global optimal solution). 1.4 Limitations and improvements of evolutionary algorithms

In today's big data era, data is large in scale and high in dimensionality. In large-scale high-dimensional optimization problems, ordinary evolutionary algorithms can easily fall into local optima, such as the high-dimensional traveling salesman problem. When the dimension of a city is greater than 100, optimization is very difficult; in feature selection problems, when the dimension of features reaches more than 10,000 dimensions, due to the redundancy and irrelevance of features, not only is it easy to fall into local optima, but the calculation time consumption is unimaginable, and optimization is basically impossible. Therefore, for large-scale data, evolutionary algorithms need to rely on effective global search technology and divide-and-conquer agent technology for problem solving.[18]. For problems falling into local optimality, scientific researchers have proposed many methods, such as the differential evolution algorithm proposed by Storn et al. in 1995.[19](Differential Evolution), like other evolutionary algorithms, DE is a stochastic model that simulates biological evolution. Through repeated iterations, individuals that adapt to the environment are preserved. However, compared with evolutionary algorithms, DE retains the global search strategy based on the population, and uses real number encoding, simple mutation operations based on differences, and one-to-one competition survival strategies to reduce the complexity of genetic operations. At the same time, DE's unique memory ability allows it to dynamically track the current search situation to adjust its search strategy. It has strong global convergence ability and robustness, and does not require the use of characteristic information of the problem. It is suitable for solving some optimization problems in complex environments that cannot be solved using conventional mathematical programming methods.[20]. In addition, of course, there are many other global optimization techniques proposed, such as the NSGA2 algorithm.[8], NSPSO and CMDPSO based on PSO[21],The above two PSO algorithms use non-dominated sorting, ,crowded distance selection, and mutation operators to ,improve the global search technology of the PSO algorithm. There are also many algorithms to solve problems that consume huge amounts of computing time. For example, there are multi-objective evolutionary algorithms based on decomposition that use the divide-and-conquer idea.[22](MOEA/D), which was proposed by Zhang Qingfu et al. in 2007. MOEA/D converts a multi-objective optimization problem into multiple scalar sub-problems, and each sub-problem consists of a uniformly distributed weight vector. For each new solution generated, the solution near the sub-problem is replaced based on the aggregation function. In addition, many researchers use cooperative coevolution algorithms (Cooperative Coevolution) and agent model-based evolutionary algorithms to improve the performance of evolutionary calculations. The main idea of ​​cooperative co-evolution is to decompose large-scale problems into groups of smaller sub-problems. The existing algorithms include CCPSO and MLCC.[23,24]. The idea of ​​the algorithm based on the proxy model is to use part of the data to proxy evaluate another part of the data or to use the proxy model to achieve the effect of approximate population evaluation to reduce computing consumption. Common ones include CPS and SMEA algorithms.[25,26]。

2. NP-hard problem

2.1.definition

Non-deterministic polynomial (English: non-deterministic polynomial, abbreviation: NP) time complexity class, including algorithmic problems that can determine whether a given solution is correct for an instance of a deterministic algorithm problem in polynomial time[17]. NP is one of the most important complexity classes in computational complexity theory. It contains the complexity class P, that is, the set of algorithmic problems that can verify whether an instance of an algorithmic problem has a solution in polynomial time; at the same time, it also contains NP-complete problems, that is, the "most difficult" problem in NP. The central issue in computational complexity theory, the P/NP problem, is to determine whether there is an effective algorithm for any NP-complete problem, or whether NP and P are equal. The formal definition of the NP problem is as follows. For a language L, L is in NP, then there exists a polynomial-time Turing machine, and a polynomial P such that:For NP-hard problems (NP-hard problems), it refers to a class of problems. It does not matter how complex they are (but it can be seen from the following discussion that they are at least NP), but as long as the problem finds a definite polynomial time solution, then we can prove that all NP problems must have a definite polynomial time solution. For NP-complete problems, if a problem is both NP-hard and NP-hard, we call it an NP-complete problem. The NP problem ranks first among the seven major mathematical problems in the world. The seven problems were carefully selected by the Scientific Advisory Committee of the Clay Mathematics Institute in the United States. The solutions to these problems will have a huge promotion effect on the development and application of mathematical theory.

2.2 Common NP problems

2.2.1 Hamiltonian cycle problem

The Hamiltonian cycle problem was proposed by astronomer William Rowan Hamilton. In a map network with multiple cities, find a path from a given starting point to a given end point that passes through all other cities exactly once.[13]. This problem differs from the famous bridge problem in that travel between certain cities is not necessarily bidirectional. For example, A→B, but B→A is not allowed. Specifically, for a given network, after determining the starting point and end point, if there is a path that passes through the network, we say that there is a Hamiltonian path in this network. The Hamiltonian path problem was finally proven to be "NP-complete" in the early 1970s. It is said that for problems of this nature, it is difficult to find an efficient algorithm. In fact, for some networks with less than 100 vertices, it will take a long time (perhaps hundreds of years) to determine whether there is such a path using the best existing algorithms and computers.

2.2.2 traveling salesman problem

The TSP problem (Traveling Salesman Problem) is one of the famous problems in the field of mathematics. Suppose there is a traveling businessman who wants to visit n cities. He must choose the path to take. The restriction of the route is that each city can only be visited once, and he must eventually return to the original city of departure. The goal of path selection is to require the path distance to be the minimum value among all paths. The TSP problem is a combinatorial optimization problem. This problem can be proven to have NPC computational complexity. There are many feasible methods to solve TSP problems, including approximate algorithms, probabilistic algorithms and parallel computing. In the approximate algorithm, given a minimization problem and an approximate algorithm, we evaluate the algorithm as follows: first give a lower bound of the optimal solution, and then compare the running results of the algorithm with this lower bound. For the maximization problem, first give an upper bound and then compare the running results of the algorithm with this upper bound. The more classic problems of approximation algorithms include: minimum vertex cover, traveling salesman problem, set cover, etc. For probabilistic algorithms, the algorithm is allowed to randomly select the next computational step during execution. In many cases, when an algorithm faces a choice during execution, a random choice is often more time-consuming than an optimal choice. Therefore, probabilistic algorithms can reduce the complexity of the algorithm to a great extent. In general, probability algorithms can be roughly divided into four categories: numerical probability algorithm, Monte Carlo algorithm, Las Vegas algorithm and Sherwood algorithm[14]. Finally, parallel computing or parallel computing is relative to serial computing. The so-called parallel computing can be divided into time parallelism and spatial parallelism, which greatly reduces the search time.

2.2.3 Feature selection problem

In the development process of feature selection, three methods have emerged, which are wrapper feature selection (wrapper), filter feature selection (filter) and embedded (embedded) method.[15]. Wrapped feature selection is a search optimization algorithm that uses classification algorithms and their performance to select subsets of features. Filter feature selection uses statistical or probabilistic properties on the data set to determine the selected features, such as information gain or symmetric uncertainty in information theory.[29], this method has less computational cost because it does not require a classifier or learning system, but its calculation accuracy is not high. The embedded method can learn features that can improve the accuracy of the model under a given model. Because wrapped feature selection can obtain a better feature subset[15,16], this paper mainly focuses on the wrapped feature selection method. There are two different types of wrapped feature selection algorithms. One is the traditional feature selection algorithm, such as SFS (sequential forward selection) and SBS (sequential backward selection). Both are greedy algorithms. The feature subset of SFS starts from the empty set and selects one feature at a time to add to the feature subset to optimize the fitness function. SBS selects one feature at a time to remove. Both feature selection algorithms are very prone to falling into local optima. The other is a feature selection algorithm based on evolutionary computation. Common ones include genetic algorithm (GA), genetic programming (GP) and algorithms based on swarm intelligence, such as particle swarm algorithm and ant colony algorithm. In recent years, due to different population mutation strategies[10,11,12]With the introduction of the feature interactive grouping strategy, the wrapped feature selection algorithm based on evolutionary computing will have better optimization results in terms of classification accuracy and number of features.

3. Solving NP difficult problems based on evolutionary computation

This section will discuss in detail the specific methods and strategies of existing evolutionary algorithms in solving NP-hard problems. Since many NP problems can be regarded as a combinatorial optimization problem, the following article will take the TSP problem and feature selection problem as examples.

3.1 TSPproblem solving

The TSP problem is a combinatorial optimization problem. This problem can be proven to have NP computational complexity. So far, no effective solution algorithm has been found for this type of problem. Therefore, we often use some approximate solution algorithms, such as genetic algorithm, ant colony algorithm, particle swarm algorithm, etc. For the genetic algorithm to solve the TSP algorithm, it needs to go through several steps of coding, initialization, selection, crossover, and mutation. Generally speaking, due to the particularity of the TSP problem in crossover mutation, symbol-based encoding is used, and we directly use paths to represent a chromosome. That is, an integer array is used. The length of the array is the number of TSP cities. Each city number is stored in the array. A sequence formed by traversing the array elements one by one starting from the subscript 0 is the path (the requirement to return to the origin is not considered here for convenience of expression, but the corresponding length will be added when calculating the path length). If you use the ant colony algorithm (ACO) to solve the TSP problem, you need to note that each ant has a Tabu table (Tabu) to store the cities it has visited, an allowed city table (Allowed) to store the cities that can still be visited, and a matrix (Delta) to store the pheromones it releases for the path it passes in a loop iteration. In addition, it also needs a state transition probability. During the search process, ants calculate the state transition probability based on the amount of information on each path and the heuristic information of the path. Of course, if there are a large number of cities in the TSP problem, it will be easy to fall into local optimality, so effective global search technology needs to be used. The specific results of solving the TSP problem will be shown in the experiment.

3.2 Solving feature selection problems

As introduced in 2.2.3 above, in addition to the filter and embedded methods for feature selection, the Wrapper method is more suitable for application evolution. When the evolutionary algorithm is applied to feature selection, real values ​​need to be converted into binary values, so a threshold needs to be preset to determine which features are selected. 0 means that the feature is not selected, and 1 means that the feature is selected. Since the two goals of feature selection are to maximize classification performance and minimize feature subsets, algorithms for optimizing feature selection problems based on multi-objectives[27,28]become the focus of research. Among them, those based on particle swarm methods include NSPSO and CMDPSO proposed by Xue Bing et al.[21]. The former algorithm applies NSGA2's non-dominated sorting and crowding distance selection to PSO; the latter uses the strategies of crowding distance and mutation dominance. These two algorithms are the first to apply the multi-objective particle swarm algorithm to feature selection. Experimental results show that NSPSO and CMDPSO can improve the search efficiency to a certain extent and can greatly reduce the size of feature subsets.

5. Conclusion

Evolutionary algorithms are widely used in many fields and have good practicality in solving NP problems. However, in many NP problems, the interactive information between different dimensions can be measured. Therefore, evolutionary algorithms cannot simply perform random optimization. In addition to heuristic functions, they should also draw on different ideas from other directions of machine learning to conduct guided optimization. Only in this way can solving NP problems have better scalability and the effect can become better.
