Understanding of maximum likelihood estimation

TechnologyMachine Learning

parameter estimation

A type of statistical inference. The process of estimating unknown parameters in a population distribution based on random samples drawn from the population. From the estimation form, it is divided into point estimation and interval estimation: from the method of constructing the estimator, there are method of moments estimation, least squares estimation, likelihood estimation, Bayesian estimation, etc.

The purpose of parameter estimation

Use the known information of the sample to infer the specific environment of the sample, that is, infer the parameter values.
For example, a bunch of discrete sample points need to be fitted, and the w coefficient of the fitted function is the inverse parameter value.
This is the essence of machine learning methods.

Maximum likelihood estimation method

Using known sample results, infer the parameter values ​​that are most likely (maximum probability) to lead to such results.
The simplest understanding of the maximum likelihood principle is that the state displayed by the sample is the state with the highest probability of occurrence among all possible states.
That is, under the condition of sample x, the estimate of parameter theta
In most cases, we estimate the result based on known conditions, and maximum likelihood estimation already knows the result, and then seeks the conditions that maximize the likelihood of the result as an estimate.
For example, Xiao Ming looks like A but not like B. Therefore, under the current conditions, we can infer that Xiao Ming and A are related by blood. This is a rough understanding of maximum likelihood estimation.
In machine learning, maximum likelihood estimation is an example of empirical risk minimization. When the model is a conditional probability distribution and the loss function is a logarithmic loss function, empirical risk minimization is equivalent to maximum likelihood estimation; similarly, maximum likelihood estimation is also the essence of logistic regression.
