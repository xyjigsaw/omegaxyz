20 tech visionaries who are creating the future

IdeasRepostsDeveloper LifeIdeas
20 technological visionaries who are creating the future (while their peers in China are still thinking about buying a house, a car, or getting married) The following is reproduced from NetEase Technology NetEase Technology News reported on April 30 that the "Wired" website recently released the 2017 Next List list, introducing 20 technological visionaries who are creating the future. Microsoft will build a more beautiful computer than Apple. The robot will 3D print cool shoes that are custom designed for you. (And it only takes a few days to be delivered to your door.) Neural networks will take over medical diagnosis, and Snapchat will try to take over the world. The people profiled in this article are visionary idealists in technology and creative fields who will push the future into your face. You may not know them yet—they're too busy working to chase the spotlight—but soon you'll be hearing their names a lot. They are the elites who are creating the future. 1. Put people first, code secondParisa Tabriz, Head of Google Chrome "Two billion people use this browser. Many of them don't speak English, and most of them don't know how the Internet works." As head of security for Google's Chrome browser, Parisa Tabriz has spent four years focusing on a very common vulnerability, one that many engineers are blind to: human nature. She pushed her 52-person team to fix what was once considered "user error." They've made some big changes in how Chrome communicates with people, like rewriting Chrome's warnings about insecure network connections so that they're readable at a level six level. Rather than relying on users to identify phishing mechanisms, the team is exploring the use of machine learning tools for automatic detection. They are beginning to pressure websites to improve their security by labeling websites that do not use the HTTPS encryption standard as "not secure." "We have been accused of being arbitrary, but we are protecting people's safety." She pointed out, "Our goal is not to solve mathematical problems, but to ensure people's safety." Tabriz's father is Iranian, and she also attaches importance to recruiting engineers from other countries, such as Iran. “You can’t keep people safe around the world if you don’t understand the challenges they face.” 2. Wall Street could run on cooperation rather than competitionRichard Craib, founder of hedge fund Numerai Wall Street has always been about capitalism. But Richard Crabb believes Wall Street can still be a friendly and cooperative place. Numerai, the San Francisco-based hedge fund he founded, relies on artificial intelligence algorithms to process all its trades. But the 29-year-old South African mathematician didn't develop these algorithms on his own. Instead, his fund crowdsources these algorithms from thousands of anonymous data scientists, who compete for Bitcoin rewards by building the most successful trading models. And that's not the weirdest part. Fundamentally, Crabb doesn’t want these data scientists to become too competitive. Whoever they are, they want to improve the fund. To encourage collaboration, Crabb developed Numeraire. It was a digital currency that would provide rewards to everyone as long as the fund was running well. Data scientists need to bet on algorithms they think will be successful. When those models work, Numeraire's value goes up for everyone. “I’m not trying to build a company or a startup or a hedge fund,” Crabb said. “I’m trying to build a country — a place where everyone works honestly together towards a common goal.” 3. Microsoft’s design will surpass Apple’sKait Schoeck, Industrial Designer, Microsoft Kate Shirk was not supposed to be working at Microsoft. She enrolled in the Rhode Island School of Design in the United States in 2009, planning to become a painter or illustrator. “I didn’t know industrial design existed,” she says. She didn't learn about industrial design until she got to school, so she changed her major, which later attracted the attention of Microsoft. The company liked her unusual portfolio—it didn't include much about computers. Today, she's one of the designers behind Microsoft's Surface products, working to help the company do something that seemed impossible for decades: outshine Apple in design. Because Shirk and his team weren't constrained by decades of PC design baggage, they were able to create whatever they came up with. “Being able to hold something we’ve made — that’s when you can tell whether it’s a good design or not,” Shirk said. In her early days at Microsoft, she and her colleagues co-created the rotating hinge that made the detachable Surface Book viable; her team also found ways to make touch-screen laptops natural and easy to use, allowing tablets to truly replace laptops and allowing traditional desktop PCs to become more like drawing desks. Thanks to designers like Schick, Microsoft's machines aren't just useful—they're beautiful. 4. Frugal science will help curb diseaseManu Prakash, Founder, Foldscope Instruments While visiting rabies clinics in India and Thailand, Manu Prakash realized a very disappointing reality: In remote villages, traditional microscopes were useless. Such equipment is cumbersome to carry and expensive to maintain; it often sits in a dusty corner of the laboratory while medical staff make visits. So the Stanford bioengineer decided to set out to build what he calls a "microscopic examination pencil"—a high-performance tool that's lightweight, durable, and cheap. In 2014, his lab launched Foldscope. It's a paper microscope that looks like an origami handicraft and can magnify objects up to 2,000 times while costing less than $1 to make. “We quickly realized that just writing scientific papers to promote it was not enough,” Prakash said. So he transformed his laboratory into a mini-Foldscope factory, giving away the microscopes to anyone who asked for one. In just one year, the lab has shipped 50,000 Foldscopes to users in 135 countries from Mongolia to rural Montana; this year it plans to ship 1 million. A team of enthusiastic DIY scientists use the tool to identify counterfeit medicines, detect faulty crops, identify counterfeit currency, and more. Earlier this year, Prakash's lab launched the Paperfuge centrifuge, which costs just 20 cents. It is a spinning toy inspired by ancient times and can be used to diagnose diseases such as malaria. Prakash's cheap but cleverly designed device proves that high-tech (and high-cost) solutions to public health problems are not necessarily the best solutions. Consider his lab's latest achievement: a way to identify a mosquito's species by recording its wing beats. What equipment is required? A flip phone. 5. TV ad budgets will be lost to SnapJeff Lucas, Vice President and Global Head of Sales, Snap In March, Snap's IPO became the third largest technology IPO to date, raising $3.4 billion. Nowadays, it is very necessary to make money. As of January 2017, the six-year-old multimedia app had lost $1.2 billion, nearly half of which occurred in 2016. Its growth rate is also slowing: In the first three quarters of 2016, it added more than 15 million new daily active users in each quarter, but by the fourth quarter, that number shrank significantly to only 5 million. So last summer the company poached media industry veteran and former Viacom sales chief Jeff Lucas. After Snap went public, he was entrusted with the important task of creating considerable profits to support the company's market value of tens of billions of dollars. To do that, he will need to fend off competitors that are heavily copying Snap, such as Instagram's Stories and WhatsApp's Status, while also taking advertising market share from Facebook and TV networks. It is reported that he is negotiating with advertising agencies including Publicis Groupe, WPP and Omnicom Group to reach a deal worth US$100 million to US$200 million. In the fierce digital advertising market, Lucas will play a very important role in winning advertiser budgets for Snap. 6. Encryption alone is not secure enoughJohn Brooks, Ricochet programmer Thanks to the popularity of messaging services like WhatsApp, Signal, and Apple's iMessage, end-to-end encryption is no longer just for spies and decryption masters; it's become almost as common as emojis. But sometimes, an impenetrable encrypted channel between sender and receiver isn't enough. Sure, it can hide the content of a message, but it can't hide the identity of the sender and recipient—metadata can reveal personal information such as membership in an organization or a journalist's network of origins. John Brooks, a 25-year-old high school dropout, has built an app that could represent the next generation of private sharing tools: tools that hide not just the content of your messages, but also the social graph of your connections. His chat app, called Ricochet, is based on anonymizing software, a feature that has kept Dark Web sites invisible and anonymous for years. However, Ricochet doesn't hide network addresses, but applies those stealth features to your PC: it turns your computer into part of the dark web. Unlike almost all other messaging apps, Ricochet allows conversations to flow directly from the sender's computer to the recipient's computer, without going through a central server that tracks a user's communication data or metadata. "The cloud will have no record of you using it," Brooks said. "It's mixed in with everything else that's going on in Tor. You're invisible to the crowd." When that invisibility becomes an option, plain old encryption starts to look extremely revealing. 7. Beginning in September this year, the executive-in-residence program will allow Silicon Valley executives to reside in some of the innovation centers in the project (a total of 18) for several years, where executives will provide advice to startups in various regions. The idea behind the program is that bringing well-connected industry leaders to these places will connect local talent to Silicon Valley and encourage startups to move into those cities. Our robots are powered by poets and musiciansBeth Holmes, Knowledge Manager, Alexa Information Team Farah Houston, Senior Manager, Alexa Personality Team Michelle Riggen-Ransom, Executive Editor, Alexa Personality Team Behind your high-tech digital assistant is the work of a team with backgrounds in a variety of liberal arts and sciences. Together, the three women shaped the personality of Amazon's Alexa digital assistant, which powers devices now used by tens of millions of consumers around the world. Michelle Regan-Ransom, who has an MFA in creative writing, compiles the robot's responses; Farrah Houston, who has an MA in psychology with a major in personality science, ensures Alexa's responses meet user expectations; and Beth Holmes, a mathematician who specializes in natural language processing, determines which recent domestic and international events, from the Super Bowl to the Oscars, should be integrated into Alexa's vocabulary. “What we have in common is that most of us have had a career in writing and have had to express humor in our writing,” Houston said. Reagan-Ransom oversees a team of playwrights, poets, fiction writers and musicians who create content that is integrated into Alexa's personality each week. (The chatbot’s temperament is loosely defined in a “personality document.”) The team then discusses the content together; most of it ends up being edited out. Alexa's personality can be pragmatic and straightforward, or whimsical and funny. The personality it shows needs to be just right, especially when discussing sensitive topics. “When we’re discussing politics, sex, or religion, our whole strategy is to change the subject with humor,” Houston said. But thanks to her female-led team, the chatbot won’t support insults. "We have been working hard to portray Alexa as confident and able to bring positive energy to people." Houston said. 9. Hard data can improve diversity issuesLaura I. Gómez, founder of Atipica Three years ago, Laura Gomez attended yet another diversity seminar in the tech world, flanked by representatives from Facebook and Google. At that time, the Hispanic entrepreneur said sternly, "This is not nepotism, we all know that. This is nepotism. A Google employee is hired by Twitter, and then hired by Facebook. Everyone is appointing their relatives and friends to hold important positions." (As someone who has worked for Twitter, YouTube and Google, she knows this very well.) This prompted Gomez to found Atipica, a recruiting software company that focuses on screening candidates based solely on skill sets. The policy may seem self-explanatory, but recruiters tend to pattern-match based on past hires—for example, favoring Google engineers who graduated from Stanford University. Atipica isn't trying to make tech CEOs feel ashamed of the makeup of their workforces; it's presenting hard data without judgment. The company's software - which pulls data from public, industry and internal sources - reveals the types of people most likely to apply for a job, analyzes hiring patterns and quantifies the probability that a particular category of candidates will accept a job offer. It also re-presents qualified candidates to new open jobs, a strategy that has resulted in thousands of candidates being contacted again by recruiters. Last fall, Atipica raised $2 million from True Ventures, Kapor Capital, Precursor Ventures and other investors. For Gomez, an undocumented Mexican immigrant until she was 18, her work at Atipica has personal meaning. “My mother worked as a nanny and housekeeper in Silicon Valley,” she said. “My voice is the voice of immigrants.” Her company’s success suggests that the tech industry’s diversity woes will be solved by data, not a series of angry tweets. 10. Music creation will move beyond the studioSteve Lacy, musician Most musicians work in studios, with engineers, producers, and dozens of contributors. Steve Lacy, on the other hand, worked in hotel rooms, or in his car, or once in a barber shop, wherever inspiration struck him. Lacy has proven to the industry through his unconventional work time and time again that good music doesn’t have to be glossy or overproduced. In February he released his first solo material, as well as a series of songs written entirely in the music creation tool GarageBand (he didn't call them albums). Lacy plugged his guitar into his iPhone's Lightning port and sang directly into the microphone. This may seem a little weird, but he's trying to prove to people that it doesn't really matter what tools you have. He is not yet a significant musician. But he is only 18 years old and is already a very popular producer. He has made rhythms with famous rapper J. Cole, rap superstar Kendrick Lamar and others. Lacy's music style is a little bit pop, a little bit soul, and a little bit R&B. He calls this style Plaid because it incorporates a lot of funky elements that you would never imagine—but somehow it just works. Even he didn't quite understand why it would work, but he knew it would. 11. When microbiology meets intelligent designChristina Agapakis, Creative Director, Ginkgo Bioworks For a biologist, Christina Agapakis has an unusual role. Her Boston biotech company, Ginkgo Bioworks, modulates yeast and bacteria to create custom organisms for everything from wine fermentation to cosmetics. In such a company, Agapakis serves as a bridge between the technical and creative departments. She works with clients such as food conglomerates to study how engineered microorganisms can be used to make their products better, cheaper and more durable. Recently, French flavor and fragrance company Robertet tapped Ginkgo’s organic designers to create a custom yeast that replicates the taste of rose flower oil. To this end, designers added the scent-producing genes from roses to yeast, allowing the yeast to produce floral-flavored compounds—without the need for expensive rose petals. Agapakis then worked with the company's spice makers to develop new spices using this novel object. “A lot of what I do is thinking creatively about what this new technology can bring,” she said. Biotech companies understand that success requires more than just good science and technology—it also requires imagination. 12. Serowski said his goal has always been to make the tech industry more responsible. There’s a DNA-based app that can help youJames Lu, Senior Vice President of Applied Genomics at Helix Advances in genetic sequencing technology mean that laboratories can now quickly and cheaply read millions of DNA-coding letters from a lump of DNA. Genomics researcher James Lu and his team at Helix (which has raised $100 million in funding led by the largest maker of DNA sequencers) are using that information to help you learn more about yourself. How exactly is it done? There is a dedicated app. First, Helix sorts and stores your entire exome—every letter of the 22,000 genes that code for proteins in your body. (The technology reveals far more data than genotypic analysis, used by companies like 23andMe, which only searches for specific markers.) Helix's partners then create apps to analyze everything from your cancer risk to your alcohol preferences. "Some people will be interested in genetic diseases, while others are more concerned about health or nutrition." James Lu said, "We work with developers to provide better products based on your genetic information." Helix's first partners include medical institutions like the Mayo Clinic and New York's Mount Sinai Hospital and National Geographic magazine. The Mayo Clinic and Mount Sinai Hospital in New York are working together to develop genetic education and health-related apps, while National Geographic is offering an app that reveals where your ancestors lived and their migration patterns, dating back 200,000 years. James Lu said that in the future, they will work with travel agencies to plan your travel itinerary based on your family tree, or work with food delivery services to customize menus that suit your metabolism. The project opens up new markets for genetic research — and a new way of self-absorption for the selfie generation. 15. Her wife was not previously in Washington. But when that time came, he couldn't leave. “My heart tells me to stay in USDS,” he wrote to his wife, who eventually joined him in Washington. As a member of the Government Technology Task Force, Katz leads a team developing an online portal for veterans. If he leaves in January, he will not be able to see the completion of two USDS projects (services to the Department of Defense and the Army). “The organization deserves someone who can help protect its mission,” Katz said. It also needs someone who can convince people in Silicon Valley that managing the president's Twitter feed isn't the government's only technology job. Katz, who avoids talking about politics, has begun recruiting friends in the tech industry, telling them that no matter who they vote for, “once you see the kinds of problems you can solve here, you tend to become addicted to them.” Through work like this, you really can (slowly) change the world. 16. Robots will make fast fashion even fasterGerd Manz, Vice President, adidas Future Team For Gen Z (generally referring to people born from the mid-1990s to after 2000), cookie-cutter styles are not good enough. They want customized designs and want a finished product quickly. "If it takes three seconds to download an app, they will be very impatient." said Gerd Manz, an industrial engineer in charge of technological innovation at Adidas. So he's leading the charge to run the company's ambitious new manufacturing facility, the Speedfactory, which employs robots instead of humans. The sports equipment giant will begin production at two Speedfactories this year, one in Ansbach, Germany, and another in Atlanta, both of which will eventually be able to produce 500,000 pairs of shoes per year, including very special designs. Thanks to advanced technologies like automated 3D printing, automated cutting and computerized knitting, a pair of shoes that may now take 18 months to develop and manufacture will soon be able to be made from scratch in just a few hours. While the Speedfactory will initially perform limited tasks, Mantz predicts the factory will eventually produce fully customized shoes. (You'll even be able to watch the entire manufacturing process of your custom shoes on video.) "Whether we're making one shoe or 1,000 pairs, the Speedfactory line will do it," Manz says. This futuristic robot factory will fulfill consumers' desires: it can produce highly customized products extremely quickly. 17. Artificial intelligence will help doctors do their jobs betterLily Peng, Product Manager, Google Brain In 2012, Google developed an artificial intelligence system that could identify cats in YouTube videos. The experiment may seem boring, but now Lily Peng is applying some of its techniques to solve a much more serious problem. She and her colleagues are using neural networks, complex mathematical systems used to identify patterns in data, to identify diabetic retinopathy, the leading cause of blindness among U.S. adults. At Google Brain, Google's main AI lab, Lily Peng fed neural networks tens of thousands of retinal scans, teaching them to "see" the small hemorrhages and other damage that are early warning signs of retinopathy. "This allows us to identify those at highest risk of developing retinopathy so we can offer them treatment as early as possible," said Lily Peng. She is an M.D. and also has a PhD in bioengineering. She's not trying to replace today's doctors - but she hopes the system will eventually help overworked doctors in poor areas around the world vastly improve the efficiency of diagnosing patients. In hospitals in India, Lily Peng is already conducting clinical trials using her AI system to analyze patient eye scans. In the future, doctors may work with AI to examine X-rays and MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) pictures to detect various diseases. "We want to expand health care coverage everywhere," says Lily Peng. Machines can help facilitate this by sharing work. 18. Microsatellites will be widely usedWill Marshall, co-founder and CEO of Planet The ultra-sensitive satellites that take stunning, high-definition photos of Earth have one major drawback: They can only capture a tiny part of the planet at a time. That's why earlier this year a company called Planet launched multiple shoebox-sized, low-cost satellites into orbit. They are capable of photographing entire continents of Earth every day – and cost far less to operate than previous satellites. Will Marshall, a former NASA scientist who co-founded Planet in 2010, said, "We want to empower a large number of people who previously did not have access to satellite imagery." Using Planet's satellite technology, relief organizations can pinpoint the worst-hit areas after a disaster; corporate farms can monitor the condition of their crops; financial companies can see how much minerals are being produced by mines; and environmental groups can track deforestation. Once you push this technology into space, you can do all kinds of exploration at a much lower cost. This will benefit companies large and small, whether they want to fly to the moon or head to Mars.
