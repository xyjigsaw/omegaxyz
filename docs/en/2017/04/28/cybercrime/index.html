Cybercrime Overview

IdeasDeveloper LifeCyber Crime
I. Concept Cybercrime It refers to the total number of offences committed by the perpetrator using computer technology to attack his system or information by means of a network, to disrupt or use the network to commit other offences. These include offences committed on the Internet by the perpetrator using his programming, encryption, decodering techniques or tools, as well as offences committed by the perpetrator using software instructions, technologies such as network systems or product encryption, and loopholes in legal provisions, both inside and outside the network, and offences committed by the perpetrator in the network system through his or her specific position as a service provider or other method. In short, cybercrime is a crime committed against and using networks, and its essence is to undermine the safety and order of networks and their information. Characteristics: Compared to conventional crime, cybercrime has a number of unique characteristics: it is low-cost, rapid and widespread; it is highly interactive, hidden and difficult to obtain evidence; it is a serious social hazard; and it is a typical computer crime. First, it is low-cost, rapid and widespread. In the case of e-mail, much less than traditional mail, especially mail sent abroad. Network development, with a few clicks on the keyboard, can send e-mails to a large number of people. In theory, the recipients are the people of the world. Secondly, it is interactive, highly hidden and difficult to obtain evidence. Network development has created a virtual computer space that both removes borders and breaks social and spatial boundaries, making possible two-way, multi-way communication. The description of everything in this virtual space is just a bunch of cold coded data, so whoever has the password is tantamount to gaining control over rights such as property and can land the site anywhere. Thirdly, there are serious social hazards. As computer information technology continues to develop, from national defence, electricity to banking and telephone systems, digital and networked at this time, the consequences will not be conceivable if these sectors are invaded and destroyed. Fourthly, cybercrime is a typical computer crime. There is now a wide range of views on what is understood by the theory of computer-related crime, in which the definition of the offence (i.e. committed by the perpetrator with a computer as a tool or as an object of attack) is more scientific. The more common crimes of pilfering, copying, altering or deleting computer data, information, spreading destructive viruses, logical bombs or placing backdoor procedures are typically computer-oriented offences, while crimes of cyber-pornography, cyber-stigmatization, defamation and intimidation, and cyber-fraud, incitement, etc., are crimes committed with virtual space created by computer networks as a tool for crime and a place for crime. The essence of cyber crime is that it undermines the safety and order of networks and their information. Composition of the crime 1. Criminal subjects The subject of the offence is the natural person or unit who has committed an act against society and is criminally liable under the law. The subject of cybercrime should be the general subject, whether natural or legal. In terms of the specific manifestations of cybercrime, the diversity of criminal subjects, the availability of cybercrime at all ages and in all professions, and the harm to society are not very different. In general, the subject of cybercrime must be an actor with a certain level of computer expertise, but a person with computer expertise cannot be considered a special subject. Although we have given engineers the title of a person with computer expertise, issued various computer-grade qualifications, etc., in the case of cybercrime, there is a significant proportion of people who are highly qualified without a certificate or title. At the same time, it should be seen that today, when computers, i.e. networks, will be increasingly demanding on so-called people with computer expertise, cybercrime will become increasingly common and it is not accurate to use criteria such as computer expertise. Moreover, the development of networks had brought new opportunities for enterprise development in e-commerce, and it should not be surprising that cyber-crime, which was the subject of legal persons, was competing for new market space. 2. Subjective aspects of crime The subjective aspect of the offence refers to the psychological attitude of the subject of the offence towards its own harmful conduct and the outcome of its harm, which includes the elements of guilt (i.e. intentional or criminal negligence) and the purpose and motive of the offence. The crime is subjectively intentional. For this type of crime, before the perpetrator enters the system, he or she needs to break through the system ' s security protection barrier by entering and exporting equipment or using technical means, using computer information networks to commit acts against society and disrupt the order of network management. This indicates the apparent criminal intent of the perpetrator, which is often direct. Even if the offence was committed in order to demonstrate its capability, the perpetrator had a clear “necessary necessity” and so on, which showed a high degree of subjective intent. 3. The object of the offence The object of computer cybercrime refers to the social relationship protected by our criminal law as a result of computer cybercrime. Since cybercrime is a means and object of crime and is not a class of crime based on the same topic as crime, the object of cybercrime is diverse. Computer cybercrime, on the one hand, causes serious damage to the management order of computer systems and, on the other, tends to directly and seriously harm other social interests. Computer cybercrime attacks complex objects, both of which are acts of aggression against two or more direct objects. The commission of computer-related crime necessarily violates the regulations of the State, thereby undermining that order. This is an objective feature of cybercrime. Objective aspects The objective aspect of the crime is an objective external factual feature of the criminal law that describes the harm caused by the act to the social relations protected by the criminal law. This is reflected in the use of computers for the commission of offences such as spying, copying, altering or deleting computer information, fraud, incitement to commit crimes, Internet pornography, and criminal network insults, defamation and intimidation. There are also violations of the security of computer systems that violate the management of computer networks, regulations, intrusions into State affairs, defence construction, sophisticated technological systems, deletions, modifications of computer information systems functions, data and procedures, or breaches of computer system software, hardware equipment, etc., which can only act as a result. This is because the perpetrator must use his/her own computer and network technology to enter a computer order on the computer through his/her own thinking, and enter into the network through the firewall (network security system), causing damage. Such cybercrime is a positive act. Classification (i) Types of offences committed on computer networks: offences of unlawful intrusion into computer information systems; offences against computer information systems. Examples include attacks on websites and online dissemination of computer viruses. VI. Development of Chinese information network security legislation It was not until 1994, when the international Internet entered China, that China introduced legislation on computers and the Internet. The first legislation was the Regulation on the Security Protection of Computer Information Systems, issued by the State Council on 18 February 1994, which was followed by the issuance of regulations on the management of computer systems and Internet security, such as the Interim Regulation on the Management of International Networking of Computer Information Networks and the Management of Security of International Networking of Computer Information Networks. With a view to regulating Internet activities and strengthening the management of Internet service providers, China promulgated regulations and sector regulations such as the Measures for the Management of Internet Information Services, the Regulations for the Regulation of Electronic Internet Announcement Services and the Regulations for the Regulation of Internet Service Establishments in China in and around 2000. In the early 1990s, computer-related crime had emerged in China and, with the widespread use of the Internet, had reached the stage of cybercrime. In order to combat the growing and changing nature of cybercrime, China amended its Criminal Code in 1997 to provide for the offences of unlawful intrusion into computer information systems and sabotage of computer information systems, making it one of the first 12 countries to enact legislation on computer-related crime; (a) The Decision on the Maintenance of Internet Security, adopted by the Standing Committee of the National People ' s Congress on 28 December 2000, which explicitly punishes 21 offences committed using the Internet in accordance with the relevant provisions of the Penal Code; Reactivation of cybercrime legislation in China in 2008 The Criminal Law Amendment (VII), which entered into force in March 2009, added three new types of cybercrime to combat new forms of cybercrime in the cyber economy. The highest judicial authority in China has also issued a number of judicial interpretations for the trial of new types of cybercrime. With regard to criminal procedure legislation, The new Code of Criminal Procedure, which entered into force in 2013, clearly defines electronic data evidence and related technical investigative measures, marking the beginning of the informationization era of the Chinese Code of Criminal Procedure. Chinese society has entered the age of the online society and the social influence of the public on the Internet is very strong, but this influence is vulnerable to abuse, with the abuse of powerful networking and search capabilities that violate citizens' rights to personal information, such as serious “cyber-violence” and “cyber manhunt”. China adopted a number of legislations to strengthen the protection of citizens ' personal information around the year 2000 and has established legal provisions protecting the personal information of citizens in different trades in the Telecommunications Regulations, the Decision on the Maintenance of Internet Security, the Insurance Act, the Law on the Prevention and Control of Infectious Diseases, the Law on Passports, the Law on Medical Practitioners, and the Law on Commercial Banks. In order to combat crimes involving serious violations of citizens' personal information, amendments (v) to the Penal Code, which entered into force in 2005, added “the offence of stealing, buying, illegally providing credit card information” and (vii) to the Criminal Code, which entered into force in 2009, added “the offence of selling, illegally supplying citizens' personal information” and “the offence of illegal access to citizens' personal information”. In response to the growing problem of violations of citizens ' personal information in Internet activities, in December 2012 China adopted the Decision on Strengthening the Protection of Information on the Internet, which sets out a system for the protection of citizens ' personal electronic information in an Internet environment. China ' s nearly 20 years of information network security legislation has been largely synchronized with the process of developing China ' s information society, and resolving the difficulties encountered in the development of information in Chinese society is the main driving force behind the legislation. As our world is in an era of internationalization and networking, China, like other countries, faces transnational cyber crime, and China's information network security legislation is in place.
