Knowledge graph embedding

A knowledge graph (KG) is a multi-relationship graph composed of entities (nodes) and relationships (different types of edges). Each edge is represented as three parts of a form (head entity, relationship, tail entity), also called a fact, representing two entities connected by a specific relationship. Although effective in representing structured data, the underlying symbolic properties of such triples often make KGs difficult to manipulate. In order to solve this problem, a new research direction-knowledge graph embedding is proposed. The key idea is to embed the components of KG, including converting entities and relations into a continuous vector space, thus simplifying operations while retaining the original structure of KG. Those entity and relation embeddings can be further applied in various tasks such as KG completion, relation extraction, entity classification and entity parsing.

Knowledge graph embedding integrating factual information

step:
①Use continuous vector space to represent entity relationships, and relationships are usually regarded as operations on vector space.
②Define a scoring function to measure the reasonableness of facts.
③Learning the representation of entity relationships, optimization problem: maximizing the rationality of global observation facts.
There are two main categories:
①Translational distance models translational distance models The former uses a distance-based scoring function
②Semantic matching models Semantic matching models The latter uses a scoring function based on similarity

Translation distance model

The translational distance model utilizes a distance-based scoring function. The plausibility of a fact is measured by the distance between two entities, usually after translation through a relationship. The figure below is a detailed explanation of TransE and its variants.

TransE and its variants

TransE: the most representative knowledge graph embedding

Entities and relations are in the same space, and for each triple (h, r, t) TransE expects: \(h+r=t\)
The scoring function is:
\(f_{r}(h, t)=-\|\mathbf{h}+\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{t}\|_{1 / 2}\)
Disadvantages: Complex relationships such as one-to-many, many-to-one, and many-to-many relationships are not applicable.
 

TransH: Solve the shortcomings of TransE in multiple relationships

An entity can have different representations under different relationships. For a triplet (h, r, t), TransH first projects the head entity vector h and tail entity vector r along the normal wr onto the hyperplane corresponding to the relationship r. They are represented by h⊥ and t⊥ as follows:
\(\mathbf{h}_{\perp}=\mathbf{h}-\mathbf{w}_{r}^{\top} \mathbf{h} \mathbf{w}_{r}, \quad \mathbf{t}_{\perp}=\mathbf{t}-\mathbf{w}_{r}^{\top} \mathbf{t} \mathbf{w}_{r}\)

TransR: entities and relations in different semantic spaces

Each relationship is associated with a specific space, and entities first need to be mapped to the relationship space.
\(\mathbf{h}_{\perp}=\mathbf{M}_{r} \mathbf{h}, \quad \mathbf{t}_{\perp}=\mathbf{M}_{r} \mathbf{t}\)
The transformation method is a projection matrix from entity space to relationship space.
Disadvantages: The conversion method is spatial projection, which is highly complex.

TransD: a simplification of TransR

Decompose TransR's projection matrix into the product of two vectors.

TransSparse: Enforcing sparsity on the projection matrix to simplify TransR

It has two versions, one is shared and the other is independent. The head and tail entities of the share version share a sparse projection matrix, while the independent head and tail entities have a projection matrix respectively.

TransM: Relax the transformation requirements of TransE and improve model performance

Assign each fact a specific relationship weight θ, changing the scoring function.
\(f_{r}(h, t)=-\theta_{r}\|\mathbf{h}+\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{t}\|_{1 / 2}\)
By assigning smaller weights to one-to-many, many-to-one, and many-to-many, the TransM model makes t farther away from h+r in the complex relationship described above.

ManifoldE

Let t be approximately located on the manifold, that is, a hypersphere with h+r as the center and radius theta_r.
 

TransF

It only needs that t and h+r are in the same direction, and h and t-r are also in the same direction.
 

TransA

A symmetric nonnegative matrix Mr is introduced for each relation r, and the scoring function is defined using adaptive Mahalanobis distance.
 

Gaussian embedding: taking into account the uncertainty of entities and relationships, using random variables for modeling

KG2E: Use Gaussian distribution to represent entities and relationships

The mean of the Gaussian distribution represents the central position of the entity or relationship in the semantic space, while the covariance of the Gaussian distribution represents the uncertainty of the entity or relationship. The KG2E model represents entities and relationships as random vectors drawn from a multivariate Gaussian distribution.
\(\begin{aligned} \mathbf{h} & \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{h}, \Sigma_{h}\right) \\ \mathbf{t} & \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{t}, \mathbf{\Sigma}_{t}\right) \\ \mathbf{r} & \sim \mathcal{N}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{r}, \mathbf{\Sigma}_{r}\right) \end{aligned}\)
Score a fact by measuring the distance between two random vectors t-h and r, measured in two ways. One is measured by KL divergence (KL distance) and the other is by calculating the inner product of probabilities.

TransG

The entity adopts Gaussian distribution, but it considers that the relationship has multiple semantics and needs to be represented by a mixed Gaussian distribution.
 

Other distance models

Unstructured model UM (Unstructured model)

Simple version of TransE, directly set all r=0
\(f_{r}(h, t)=-\|\mathbf{h}-\mathbf{t}\|_{2}^{2}\)

Structured embedding SE (Structured embedding)

Project the head and tail entities for each relation r by using two separate matrices
\(f_{r}(h, t)=-\left\|\mathbf{M}_{r}^{1} \mathbf{h}-\mathbf{M}_{r}^{2} \mathbf{t}\right\|_{1}\)
