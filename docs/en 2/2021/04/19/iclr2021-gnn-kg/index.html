Theory

HOW NEURAL NETWORKS EXTRAPOLATE: FROM FEEDFORWARD TO GRAPH NEURAL NETWORKSThe authors study how neural networks trained via gradient descent perform inference, i.e. what they learn beyond the support of the training distribution. **It is known that MLP cannot perform inference well in some simple tasks, but graph neural networks (structured networks with MLP modules) can handle more complex tasks. Through theoretical explanation, the article determines the conditions under which MLP and GNN can perform good inference. When analyzing the successes and limitations of GNNs, the authors propose a hypothesis: The success of GNNs in extrapolating algorithmic tasks to new data (such as larger graphs or edge weights) relies on encoding task-specific nonlinear architecture or features.Interpreting Graph Neural Networks for NLP With Differentiable Edge MaskingThe authors introduce a post hoc analysis method to explain GNN predictions that can identify unnecessary edges. Given a trained GNN model, we will learn a simple classifier that predicts for each edge in each layer whether it can be discarded. Conclusion: The article shows thatWithout degrading model performance, a large proportion of edges can be discarded, leaving the remaining edges to explain model predictions.   Expressive Power of Invariant and Equi-variant Graph Neural NetworksIn this paper, the authors propose a theoretical framework that enables comparison of the expressive capabilities of different GNN architectures. The current universality theorem only applies to the intractable class of GNNs. Here, the authors demonstrate the first approximate guarantee for practical GNNs, with theoretical results demonstrated for invariant GNN computational graph embeddings (Node replacement of the input graph does not affect the output) and equivariant GNN compute node embeddings (A permutation of the input results in a permutation of the output）。   On the Bottleneck of Graph Neural Networks and its Practical ImplicationsBottlenecks of GNN:The inability to aggregate information over long paths causes exponentially growing information to be compressed into fixed-size vectors. As a result, when the prediction task relies on remote information interaction, information from distant nodes cannot be disseminated.In this article, the author proves the above phenomenon and compares the results caused by such bottlenecks in different GNN models.Analyzing the Expressive Power of Graph Neural Networks in a Spectral PerspectiveBy bridging the gap between spectral and spatial designs of graph convolutions, this paper analyzes the expressive capabilities of graph neural networks in the spectral domainGraph Information Bottleneck for Subgraph RecognitionGraph information bottleneckOn Graph Neural Networks versus Graph-Augmented MLPsThe relationship between graph neural network and graph enhanced MLPGraph Edit NetworksThe author demonstrated that GNN can predict graph edit distance

Model

Graph-Based Continual LearningContinuous learning models can still cause the model to "forget" when training on incrementally available data from non-stationary distributions. The Rehearsal approach alleviates this problem by maintaining and repeating small segments of previous samples, often implemented as independent arrays of memory slots. The authors propose augmenting such arrays with a learnable random graph that captures pairwise similarities between its samples and is used not only to learn new tasks but also to prevent forgetting.Graph Convolution with Low-rank Learnable Local FiltersDeep models using graph convolutions such as GCN provide a suitable framework for processing non-Euclidean data, but these models based on the global graph Laplacian operator lack the expressiveness to capture the local features required for signal representation on non-Euclidean grids. This paper introduces a novel graph convolution model with learnable low-rank local filters that is provably more expressive than previous spectrogram convolution methods.Graph Traversal with Tensor Functionals: A Meta-Algorithm for Scalable LearningAmong the algorithms for graph representation learning, the "message passing" method and the "node embedding" method operate differently, but these methods all utilize the structural information of the graph, so the author hopes to learn them approximately through random graph traversal. The article proposes a unified meta-algorithm framework that uses Tensor Functionals (GTTF) for graph traversal, which can simplify the implementation of various graph algorithms and achieve transparent and effective scaling of large graphs. This algorithm has two functions that can be input, and simply changing these two functions can make it specialized for obtaining various GRL models and targets.How to Find Your Friendly Neighborhood: Graph Attention Design with Self-SupervisionThe author proposes a self-supervised graph attention network (SuperGAT) for noisy images. SuperGAT will gain more attention when distinguishing neighbor nodes on wrong edges.Discrete Graph Structure Learning for Forecasting Multiple Time SeriesThe authors propose a graph neural network approach that learns graph structures to enhance predictions of multiple multivariate time series when the graph is unknown.Adaptive Universal Generalized PageRank Graph Neural NetworkGNN model based on PageRank to solve the over-smoothing problemBoost then Convolve: Gradient Boosting Meets Graph Neural NetworksIt combines the advantages of GBDT and GNN to solve node-level prediction problems on graphs with tabular data.Directed Acyclic Graph Neural NetworksThis paper proposes DAGNN, a graph neural network designed for directed acyclic graphs, which outperforms conventional GNNs by leveraging partial ordering as a strong inductive bias along with other suitable architectural features.Combining Label Propagation and Simple Models out-performs Graph Neural NetworksThis paper shows that for many node classification problems, shallow models that ignore the structure of the graph can outperform state-of-the-art GNNs by combining them with two simple post-processing steps that exploit the label structure: (i) "error correlation", which disperses residual errors in the training data to correct errors in the test data; and (ii) "prediction correlation", which smoothes predictions on the test data.Simple Spectral Graph ConvolutionA simple and effective graph convolution method based on Markov diffusion kernel, suitable for unsupervised, semi-supervised and supervised tasks

KG

RNNLogic: Learning Logic Rules for Reasoning on Knowledge GraphsThis paper studies how to learn logical rules for reasoning on KG. Logic rules are critical for learning because they are interpretable when making predictions and generalize to other tasks. Existing methods have large search spaces (e.g., neural logic programming), or very sparse rewards (e.g., reinforcement learning-based techniques), resulting in ineffective optimization. This paper proposes a probabilistic model called RNNLogic.RNNLogic treats logical rules as latent variables and uses logical rules to train rule generators and inference predictors simultaneously. RNNLogic isBased on EM algorithm. In each iteration, the inference predictor is updated to explore some generated logical rules for reasoning. Then in the E-step, a set of high-quality rules is selected from all generated rules through posterior inference. Then in the M-step, the rule generator is updated with the rules selected in the E-step.Explainable Subgraph Reasoning for Forecasting on Temporal Knowledge GraphsThis paper provides a link prediction framework that leverages subgraphs for reasoning and modeling structural dependencies and temporal changes. The author proposes a temporal relational attention mechanism and a new reverse representation update scheme,to guide the formulation around the closed subgraph of the query.Pick. The subgraph is extended through iterative sampling of temporally adjacent points and an attention propagation mechanism. Compared with previous time series KG prediction methods, our model achieves up to 20% improvement on Hits@1. A survey of 53 respondents was also conducted, showing that the evidence extracted by the model for link prediction is consistent with human understanding.Learning to Deceive Knowledge Graph Augmented Models via Targeted PerturbationKnowledge graphs improve the performance of various knowledge-intensive tasks such as question answering and item recommendation. Such KG models can also "explain" the KG information most relevant to predictions. In this paper, the authors question whether these models do indeed live up to our expectations. The article shows that through reinforcement learning strategies (or even simple heuristics), one can produce deceptively perturbed KGs that significantly deviate from the semantics and structure of the original KG while maintaining the downstream performance of the original KG. **The author's findings cast doubt on the ability of KG-augmented models to reason about KG information and give reasonable explanations.Interpreting Knowledge Graph Relation Representation from Word EmbeddingsMany models perform representation learning by exploiting the low-order latent structure of KG data, encoding known relationships between entities and being able to infer unknown facts. Although their predictive performance has been steadily improving, the underlying structure that explains how such models capture semantic information remains unexplained. Based on the latest theoretical understanding of word embeddings, the authors classify KG relations into three types, and each type derives specific requirements for generating their representations.
